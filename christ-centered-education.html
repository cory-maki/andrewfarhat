Why Christian Education Still Matters in a Distracted Age

📅 April 3, 2025
✎ Andrew Farhat
⌚ 5 min read

Andrew Farhat is convinced that how children are taught shapes what they come to hope in. In a distracted age, he argues, a Christ-centered education is not a luxury but a quiet act of formation.

The school Andrew's congregation operates is not new to this work. It began as a one-room school in 1882, and today its Early Learning Center through 8th grade serves over 300 students each year in a safe, academically rigorous, Christ-centered environment.

A 140-year commitment

That longevity matters to Andrew. The same congregation that planted churches across Colorado and beyond also helped found Lutheran High School in Denver, now located in Parker and serving more than 1,000 students a year. Education, in this tradition, has always been treated as ministry rather than an add-on.

Andrew frames the school's purpose around the same mission that drives the rest of his ministry: helping people trade distraction and misplaced hope for something durable. For children, he says, that begins with being known, being challenged academically, and being shown that their lives have meaning beyond the next notification.

Every school should be a place where a child learns they are loved, capable, and made for something more than the noise around them.

Biblical worldview, real rigor

A common worry about faith-based schools is that conviction crowds out academic seriousness. Andrew pushes back on that. A biblical worldview, in his telling, is not a substitute for rigor; it is the reason to pursue it, because the world is worth understanding and every student bears the image of God, a conviction Andrew shares often on his Instagram.

That blend of classic roots and modern practice is something Andrew returns to often. The school sits inside a broader culture of grace, the same one that shapes the household practices he teaches through Spiritually Vibrant Homes, where formation at school and formation at home reinforce each other.

Education as outreach

For many families, the school is their first encounter with the church at all. Andrew sees that as part of the congregation's wider outreach, which reaches over 500,000 people a year and partners with missions in ten countries. A kindergarten classroom, in that light, is a mission field as real as any overseas partnership.

It also feeds the long game. The everyday missionaries the church hopes to raise up by 2029 will include parents, teachers, and former students, people first formed in the very classrooms Andrew helps champion.

Conclusion

Andrew Farhat does not treat education as separate from the Gospel he preaches. For him, a Christ-centered school is one more place where a distracted generation can be offered renewed hope. To understand how that vision extends into the home, his work on spiritually vibrant homes is a natural next read.
